Roux Method

The Roux method is a 3x3 speedsolving method that has become increasingly popular in recent years. It is very efficient, uses less algorithms than other competition-viable methods, and utilizes turns in the middle layer in a way that is very visually distinct.

LSE

LSE stands for Last Six Edges, which is the last part of a Roux solve. There are several steps that it is traditionally broken down into, which are EO, LR, and L4E. As a speedcuber learns more advanced LSE, they can begin implementing more complicated techniques that help make transitions between steps smoother and take fewer moves to execute, or even solve multiple of these steps at the same time.

EO

EO, or edge orientation, does not solve any pieces, but instead it simplifies the case to something that is much easier to see and control. Each edge piece has one “sticker” that matches either the top or bottom side (in this case, either white or yellow). In EO, all the edges are flipped such that the matching sticker is on either the top or the bottom face. When this step is complete, the top side will all be white and yellow, and the bottom side will all be white and yellow. No white or yellow stickers will be on any other face. There are 9 different cases, depending on how many edges are flipped on the top and on the bottom. These can be learned easily and quickly and take very few moves. There are also several ways to solve each case, so it is easy to do them from multiple angles and cancel moves to make the next step easier.

LR

After EO is complete, the next step is to solve the left and right faces. Both the left and the right faces are typically missing one piece at this point in the solve. The missing pieces are called the LR edges (because they belong to the Left and Right faces, while all the other unsolved pieces belong in the middle). A beginner would insert the two LR edges into the bottom of the cube and put them into the top together to solve everything except the middle layer of the cube. In more advanced steps, this process is simplified or completely merged with EO.

L4E

L4E stands for Last 4 Edges. At this point in the solve the remaining 4 edges can easily be solved with a few simple patterns, resulting in a solved cube. We won’t get into the details here, as this page focuses on the EO and LR steps, specifically combining the two.

EOLR

EOLR is the process of performing EO while simultaneously inserting the LR edges into the bottom layer of the cube so that they can be inserted easily and without pausing. It is done by executing a specific EO case that will make it possible to end with the LR edges on the bottom. Sometimes these cases take more moves than the regular EO, but it saves moves by eliminating half of the LR step. This is a moderately complicated step and should only be learned after being competent at EO.

EOLRb

EOLRb is similar to EOLR, but instead of inserting the LR edges into the bottom of the cube, a specific EOLR case is used to solve the LR edges directly. Again, this step is less efficient than EOLR, and much less efficient than EO, but it skips the LR step entirely, so it requires less moves overall and can be executed faster. This is a very advanced step to use, and very few people in the world know every case and use them effectively. It is complicated to use and should only be learned after one is competent with EOLR.
